# Paleontological roots

<div class="box">

<div class="columns" style="padding:15px">
	<div class="column is-three-quarters">

<h3> History in a nutshell</h3>
<p> 
The alliance has its roots in paleontological data analysis. Quantitative paleontology has become a mature digital discipline thanks to digital databases that allow the aggregation of data from a variety of sources, including fossil occurrences, stratigraphy, and taxonomy. This discipline has its roots in biodiversity studies, with the primary questions focusing on the details of how life has reached the incredible diversity that we see today.
</p>
<p>
Efforts to evaluate the biodiversity of life throughout Earth history date to at least 1860. At this time, British geologist [John Phillips](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Phillips_(geologist)) published a compilation of fossil data from the locally available record. This first diversity curve showed features including a general increase in diversity towards the Recent and multiple large declines, representing mass extinction events. Evidence from large data compilations have continued to build on this foundation, as the idea of and evidence for mass extinctions remains a major contribution of paleontology to general scientific understanding.
</p>

More recent efforts to reconstruct the history of life on Earth have benefitted tremendously from expanded data coverage and quality. Famously, [Jack Sepkoski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Sepkoski) compiled what would become known as the “Sepkoski Curve” in the 1980s. This effort, which used the [Treatise on Invertebrate Paleontology](https://journals.ku.edu/InvertebratePaleo) and focused on marine mollusks, further divided the record by identifying five mass extinctions and three evolutionary faunas. The curve continues to be a cornerstone of paleontology, with researchers often recreating it and testing hypotheses drawn from it. The coverage and quality of data used continue to improve, as does our awareness of biases in the data we use. Much work remains to address these shortcomings, especially where the legacies of human actions like colonialism  and socioeconomic inequity loom large.


</div>

</div>

<div class="columns">
<div class="column" markdown="1">
As more paleontological research is conducted, our inferences continue to be refined and now are used to evaluate taxonomic extinction risk, susceptibility to climate change, and human effects on ecosystems. Such research is only possible with the sharing of information, with researchers sharing their knowledge, data, and results. With the advent of the internet, community-curated resources (e.g., [Paleobiology Database](https://paleobiodb.org/#/), [Neotoma Paleoecology Database](https://www.neotomadb.org/)) have become standard platforms enabling quantitative analyses of life on Earth. Even as these exceptional resources allow researchers to ask new and bigger questions, they often exist in isolation. Now, with current and emerging data tools and technologies, we seek to unite and grow these community-curated resources to enable new insights about the history of life on Earth.
</div>

## Researching the Coevolution of Life and Earth

When it comes to current research, traditional disciplinary boundaries are beginning to break down as the gaps between traditional disciplines are getting filled with cross-disciplinary studies. These studies necessarily utilize data that come from various traditional backgrounds, across all Earth Systems.
Better understanding the history of how the abiotic environment influenced life in the geological past, and vice versa, is essential if we are to understand how we, humans, have been and are influencing our environment and the ecosystems that we rely on for sustenance and survival. The tools utilized for this historic endeavour frequently overlap with the tools needed for studies focused on the modern, making the temporal separation between past and modern research focus increasingly obsolete. The boundaries between macroecological, macroevolutionary and paleontological, and paleoclimatological study are past: we stand united in the face of how much we do not understand about the incredible complexity of Life on Earth and their interaction.
Why paleontology as the basis? Understanding the distribution of life on Earth today is burdened with many complexities, which is even more difficult to tackle when we try to use fossils. Getting to the simple parameters like where, when and which taxon lived on the surface of Earth is the result of a complex modelling process that is ultimately a model of Earth’s geological past.
The research community of Earth-Life interaction is in deep need for a globally-integrated data system that is, openly accessible, [FAIR](https://www.go-fair.org/fair-principles/), equitable, distributed and democratized.
The cost of such an infrastructure is beyond what we currently can attain.

# The Paleosynthesis project

<div class="columns">

<div class="column is-four-fifths" markdown="1">

The [Paleosynthesis Project](https://www.paleosynthesis.nat.fau.de/) was conceptualized around a need to unify the field of paleontology by building a shared vision of current and future research priorities. Working groups of the Paleosynthesis have come together to identify Big Questions in paleontology, evaluate the current status of data infrastructure, and synthesize data across commonly used databases spanning neontological and paleontological timescales. Through these efforts, a common set of priorities have been established and many lessons have been learned about the current state of paleontological research. Pertinently, it has become clear that the prevailing tendency for academics to work in isolation has produced a fragmented data ecosystem with redundant, incompatible resources.

</div>

<div class="column is-one-fifth" markdown="1">

As the first Paleosynthesis working group, [BioDeepTime](https://biodeeptime.github.io/) aimed to evaluate community turnover across modern and paleontological timescales by compiling and harmonizing biodiversity time series data. Although ultimately only including a small percentage of available data, the process of attempting this compilation was highly instructive. Even though data held in biodiversity databases is fundamentally similar (i.e., counts or occurrences of taxa in a given place, time, and environment), the metadata and data reporting do not align intuitively and require expert-level experience with each database in order to compile data across them. 
Much as previous efforts (e.g., ePANDA, Earth-Life Consortium) have attempted to build a united data ecosystem, we have reinvigorated this objective through the Integrated Record of Ancient Life (IRAL) working group. This exploratory effort quantified previous databasing work and helped to establish a foundation on which to build. BioDeepTime, IRAL, and other Paleosynthesis projects, repeatedly have taught us one thing: this is a large undertaking and it will take a dedicated community to make it a reality. As we move into the next phase of this work, we invite you to join us!
